# Welcome to the Bench: The Magic Fishing Reel
An oddball experiment I couldn’t resist

## First Impressions

I was doing a late‑night auction scroll, looking for some oddball fly reels I'd seen in a *Reel News* article, like a Stuart "Aberdeen" or a Precisionbilt "Mosquito," but then there was a listing that made me stop: a real oddball, a “Magic Fishing Reel" sitting well below my semi-strict "less than $50" rule with enough other bids to make it interesting and just enough mystery to reel me in like a speckled trout. I thought, "*If I ever got to invite Captain Beefheart to go fishing sometime, this is the reel I would give hime to use*."

Now it’s here, freed from its really carefully packed shipping box, on the workbench. First impression: It's HUGE, like twice the size of any other reel in my collection, about the size of a tuna can, and actually of a very similar appearance. A good 10 centimeters across. In the ad some of the original packaging was quoted as saying something like "it will spin, fly cast, baitcast . . ." basically someone's idea of a sort of swiss army knife of a reel. Unfortunately, things that try to do everything instead of just doing one thing well often do everything in a sort of not-great way, and I suspect that, in conjuction with its massive size and lack of design aesthetics (again, think "tuna can") probably account for its short time on the market. Still, just the name . . . the fact that someone, sometime decided to make a "Magic Fishing Reel," makes it worth *something*, at least as a curiosity. But since my personal obsession is finding mid-priced, mid-century American reels and making them usable again, I also want to take it apart, see what it needs, and take it out on the water, even if it's just once to see what the fish think of it.

I didn’t need another reel right now. I have four or five (or maybe half a dozen, but who's counting?) others ahead of it in the queue to be serviced. But I did need, in some visceral way, to know what, exactly, made this one *magic*.

## What Makes This One Different

This isn’t a classic Zebco 33 or a well‑loved workhorse from the usual suspects like Penn or Shakespeare or South Bend, or even Pflueger. It’s one of those in‑between inventions that show up now and then in reel history—part experiment, part marketing story, part “what if we tried this?” Actually, probably a lot of that last bit.

From the outside, you can see the designers reaching for a trick: a feature that would make this reel stand out in a crowded catalog. At least part of the magic was that it was supposed to do what a whole boxful of ordinary reels could do. Why have a fly reel, a baitcaster, and a spinner, when you could just have one *magic* reel? Well, actually there are a lot of very good reasons to have different reels for different purposes, but we are not entirely talking common sense here, we are talking about, well, magic.

Whatever. What I’ve actually bought is the end result of some mid-century inventor's flight of fancy: a single reasonably well-preserved example of a reel that lived a whole small life and somehow survived long enough to land on my bench. Our destinies, as it were, intersected. And that may be just enough magic for now.

## On the Bench Today

Right now the Magic Fishing Reel (coincidentally or not, manufactured by the, ah, Magic Fishing Reel Company of Denver, Colorado) is sitting on the little box that serves as an altar of sorts for reels to pose for their "hero" shots; for the moment, the center of attention. The handle has some beautifully intriguing red translucent knobs on each end and there is a lever of some sort that hinges outward for no purpose that I have yet determined--but that's a large measure of why I'm doing this. What is that thing for? I've never seen anything like it on any other reel.

The finish is worn in a few spots, but not abused. It's made of good mid-century American steel, so you could probably drop it off the back of a truck, go back for it, run over it in the process, and still fish with it. I have a very soft spot for the quality and workmanship that went into even the strangest industrial products from that era. It looks like it’s been fished a bit, maybe set aside when it didn’t quite live up to its promise, then dropped into a box and forgotten.

I haven’t opened it up yet to see what makes it tick. After I post about this one it's going to the end of the queue to wait its turn to be serviced--unless I just can't stand it one day and have to take it apart. For now, I’m just taking in the way it feels, turning it over in my hands and letting questions stack up. Why so enormous? What is that bizarre handle thing? What was the last fish caught with it?

## Hopes and Suspicions

Part of me hopes this is one of those under‑appreciated designs that turns out to be better than its short market life suggests, although right away I can see why it wouldn't sell. Huge. All the design aesthetics of a tuna can. Maybe the magic is *real* in a small, practical way, something that makes sense once you see it from the inside out. One bit of magic has already surfaced in its documentation: As I cast about for a schematic, it turned out the US Patent Office site had the original patent, and that had a schematic--and weirdly, it was patented on my birthday, just a few years before I was born. So we clearly have some sort of bond. Weirdly.

Another part of me pretty much knows I’m going to find exactly what first impressions hinted at: an interesting idea that didn’t quite translate into a successful design. A reel that tried to do something new and ended up being quickly displaced by simpler, more practical, more elegant designs.

Either way, that’s part of the fun. This reel doesn’t *have* to be like any other reel to be interesting, and in fact that is *exactly* why it's interesting. It just has to tell the truth about what it was trying to be, and so we will have to get to know each other on the workbench.

## What Comes Next

Next up is the first real session on the bench: side plates off, a look at the gearing, a closer listen to whatever that hesitation is when the handle turns. I’ll see how much actual magic shows up in the engineering, and whether it can be brought back to fishable shape. But that won't happen until I get those other six or eight reels serviced. Either way, I don't think the fish have much to worry about.

If there’s a real story inside—good, bad, or just strange—it’ll probably earn a "Welcome to the Bench" post where we take it apart and maybe, if it behaves on the water, a future "Reel Story." For now, it’s here on the bench, waiting for me to discover what kind of magic is actually inside.
