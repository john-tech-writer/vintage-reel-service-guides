Spincasters, spinners, and baitcasters, oh my!

Once I started working on the old Zeb, I began to realize it was going somewhere, I just didn't know where. Or even what IT was. There definitely wasn't a plan. Well, like Marie Kondo says, something like,

*when you get rid of enough of your stuff, you start to realize what your life is all about.*

And that's sort of what happened.

For one thing there was also my Dad's old reel, also rescued from a box in the toolshed - an old Pflueger Pelican spinner, which hadn't worked or seen the water in decades either. I always felt very grown-up when I tried to use that one as a kid. Another story for another day.

After a lot of hacking I figured out there was a part in the Zeb that just was not coming back, so I started to haunt eBay for a Zeb of similar vintage to use for parts. [possible revision: It wasn't easy to find a Zebco 33 that was of a similar vintage, and they definitely were not all alike.] That's how I ended up with the "Hudson Bay Red" variant, which I still can't explain, but that's a post for another day. At any rate I did trade a part from Red to my old Zeb and it worked. I put new line on it (after spending quite a bit of time understanding why line twist was bad and how to not do that) and took it fishing for the first time since who knows when. I can't remember just at this moment whether or not I caught anything but that seems almost irrelevant.

[possible revision: ...but that seems almost irrelevant. I thought, "I only have so much time, space, and mental bandwidth. How can I focus all this? It was working again, and I had made it work again, and I was back out on the water enjoying the sand and salt. There were little fish in the water around my feet. There were boats and gulls. Something came together in that time-space that had been scattered for a long time.

Oh and the tech writing thing

Spincasters, spinners, and baitcasters—suddenly they weren’t just clutter. They were the start of a project. And as I have discovered, or really already knew, I love projects. I love work. I spent almost 20 years as a corporate technical writer, mostly software, but also industrial furnaces and just about everything else. Then I taught writing for almost 10 years. I’m still working out what you can do with 26 abstract symbols.

I may not have always loved my jobs (and definitely not all my managers) but stragely, perhaps, I always loved tech writing. The aiming for precision. Figuring things out. See that technical literature is just as literary as any other literature. Learning new things and explaining them, even if nobody ever read the manuals.

As I think Jerry Garcia said once, and significantly,

*Follow your weird.*

And picking up that old Zeb for the first time in decades certainly has infused some mysterious "it" with a life of its own.

Well, so then I asked Perplexity AI [link] (yet another story I think, for another day) what was the best way to create an online documentation library for hosting illustrated procedures about servicing old fishing reels. Well of course that’s the first thing any good would-be vintage reel repair aficionado would do, right? Well, probably not. But that’s just me. And that’s how I started having to figure out GitHub [link] . And MkDocs [link]. And Mermaid [link] (ironically).

And now I have Vintage Reel Service Guides [link], a GitHub repository. And so on. A few months ago (roundabout January of 2026 as I recall) I didn’t even know what a repository was.

And oh yes if I wanted to link some simple videos... well, yes, of course a YouTube channel [link] . And for the narrative content, like actual fishing trips, and reminiscences, philosophical musings, ah well, Substack...

I guess it’s kind of drawn me in. Like I said, there was no plan. Now I have one--I have five online volumes planned that will take years to finish... I don’t get out much to fish these days, mostly I am refactoring my repository  (better not to ask), organizing my library of templates, figuring out the finer points of 3-point lighting for benchtop workspace photography. Making the reels look good, like the technical art objects they are.

The fish can wait. They have their fishy business, and I have mine.

Well, I never lack for ideas. I found out about and joined the Old Reel Collector’s Association (ORCA) [link] so now I read The Reel News every month. I currently have a backlog of reels waiting patiently to be taken apart and their innards documented, gear by gear. Every time I read one of their articles I see another oddball vintage reel, like a Precisionbilt “Mosquito,” that I just have to have some day to take apart, and to fish with. Because it has clear plastic sides. So you can see the gears. And why would you not want to see the gears?

I have enough to keep me busy for the next couple of decades.

Why it matters

There’s definitely a nostalgia of sorts for the American craftsmanship of the era when I was growing up, the early 60s. We still made the best stuff on the planet right here on our own continent. These reels are definitely a symbol of that. They are so incredibly well-thought-out and well-made; I take apart a reel that’s maybe 50 to 70 years old, clean the gummy grease and sand off the gears, and it’s as good as it ever was. How many things made today will be like that in 50 years? Or even 5?

The materials are of a quality that would be difficult to obtain now, just the simple well-made, American-made stainless steel that still looks almost new after 50 years of fishing. I took the Zeb apart with a pipe wrench (maybe not best practices, that was the only way to get it apart) and it didn’t put a scratch on it. My grandfather worked in a steel mill. So many connections.

Things that are that well-made deserve to be shined up and used, not placed reverently on a shelf to only be admired. They are supposed to go out with us and get salty and sandy and wet, and have a day on the water, with friends, or with just the wind and the birds and the fish, if they choose to show up.

Things like this remind me that we used to make things well, and if it was possible once, it is possible again. Something as simple as a fishing reel can really make you think, about roads taken and not taken, things lost, things forgotten, things that may yet be regained and remembered.

Craftsmanship is good. Fixing things is good. Things that can be fixed are good. Teaching others how to fix things is good.
