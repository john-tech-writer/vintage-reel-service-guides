# Setting up multiple publications for one substack account

## Step‑by‑step: Create your Vintage Reel Substack

1. Log into your existing Substack account as usual.
2. Click your profile icon (top right) and go to **Account** or **Settings**.
3. On the account Settings page, scroll down until you see the “Publications” section.
4. Click “Create another publication.” This starts the setup flow for a brand‑new Substack under the same login.
5. Enter the name for your new publication (for example, “Vintage Reel Club”), choose the URL (like `vintagereel.substack.com`), and add a short description of what it’s about.
6. Finish the basic setup (logo, color, paid/free settings, etc.), then click through to your dashboard.

Once this is done, you’ll have two separate publications under one personal account, and you can switch between them from your profile menu.  Each will have its own homepage, subscriber list, and posts.

## Alternative: a “Vintage Reel” section instead

If you’d rather keep everything under one main Substack but separate out the vintage‑reel content, you can create a new **Section** instead of a second publication:

1. Go to your current publication’s **Settings**.
2. Click **Sections** in the left sidebar.
3. Click “Add section,” name it something like “Vintage Reels,” and decide whether people can subscribe to that section separately.

This keeps your audience in one place but lets readers choose whether they get the vintage‑reel posts.

## Ongoing series structure

You could run three loose series side by side:

1. **Bench Notes** (behind the scenes, like the opener)
    - Short posts about specific repairs, odd problems, or a cool tool you relied on.
    - Occasional light mentions of the GitHub repo: “here’s the cleaned‑up version of today’s notes if you’re curious.”
2. **Service Guides** (the more structured, practical posts)
    - Each guide = one reel model, with sections: overview, common issues, tools, steps, gotchas, and link out to the repo.
    - Think “reference that’s still readable in email.”
3. **Reel Stories** (field / fishing outing pieces)
    - Narrative posts about using a reel you’ve restored, the day on the water, what worked and what didn’t.
    - These can be lighter and more story‑driven, still tying back to the gear.

You don’t need a rigid template, but keeping these three “lanes” in mind will help readers know what they’re opening that day.

## Detailed outline: “At the Bench”

You can paste this straight into a Substack draft and write into the headings.

1. **Cold open: one reel, one moment**
    - Start with a single sentence image: a reel on the bench, parts laid out, or the sound of a clicker after it’s fixed.
    - 2–3 sentences on why you care about keeping these old reels alive.
2. **Photo: the bench**
    - Full‑width image of your bench setup.
    - Short caption: what’s in frame (vise, tools, a reel mid‑tear‑down, maybe a notepad).
3. **Section: What happens on this bench**
    - Brief, plain‑language walkthrough of your process:
        - A reel arrives (where it came from, what’s wrong).
        - Initial inspection and “listening” to the problem.
        - Tear‑down, cleaning, and re‑assembly.
        - Testing and photographing.
    - Keep this conversational, not technical—like explaining it to a curious fishing buddy.
4. **Section: From bench notes to “service guides”**
    - Explain that you take messy bench notes first (paper notebook, scratch pad, voice memos).
    - Then you clean them up into a repeatable checklist or set of steps.
    - Light mention: “Those live in a GitHub repo for the folks who like structured, versioned notes—but you don’t have to know Git to follow along here.”
    - One or two sentences about why you like keeping a public record (saving knowledge, helping others fix their own reels).
5. **Section: What you’ll find in this publication**
    - Brief bullet list describing the three “lanes”:
        - Bench Notes – small repair stories and odd problems.
        - Service Guides – more structured how‑to posts for specific models.
        - Reel Stories – actual days on the water with the reels you’ve brought back.
    - Mention that “A Reel with a Sticky Clicker” is coming next as the first Bench Notes post.
6. **Section: How often and how to follow along**
    - A realistic cadence (e.g., “aiming for roughly twice a month to start”).
    - Invite readers to hit subscribe and reply/comment with reels they’d love to see covered.
7. **Closing: why this matters (to you)**
    - 3–5 sentences about why these old reels feel worth the time: stories they carry, how they fish, the satisfaction of making them sing again.
    - End with a simple, grounded line like: “Next up: a reel with a sticky clicker.”

## Outline: “A Reel with a Sticky Clicker”

This one reads like a short story with a practical spine.

1. **Opening: how you met this reel**
    - 1–2 sentences: where the reel came from (a friend, a shop, your own stash) and the first moment you noticed the sticky clicker.
    - Describe the bad behavior in sensory terms: delayed click, uneven resistance, or the way it feels under your thumb.
2. **What a clicker is supposed to do (plain language)**
    - 2–3 sentences, no jargon: what the clicker does on a reel and why it matters on the water.
    - One simple line for beginners: “If you’re new to this, the clicker is the little mechanism that gives you that reassuring, ratchety sound when line goes out.”
3. **On the bench: tracing the problem**
    - Short sequence of what you checked, in narrative form:
        - Opened the side plate, looked for obvious dirt or old grease.
        - Checked springs, pawls, and contact points.
        - Any visual “aha” moment (gunk, wear, misalignment).
    - Include 1–2 photos: “before cleanup” view and a close‑up of the problem area.
4. **What actually fixed it**
    - Keep this mid‑level, not a full service guide:
        - “In the end, it came down to…” (e.g., hardened grease, a tired spring, a worn tooth).
        - Bullet the basic steps you took: clean, lube, adjust/replace, reassemble.
    - One clear takeaway: “If your clicker feels like this, here’s the first place I’d look.”
5. **Bench Notes vs. full Guide**
    - 2–3 sentences: this post is the story and the big idea; the more detailed, step‑by‑step guide for this model will live as a Service Guide with a link once it’s written.
    - Light mention of the GitHub repo as the “deep documentation” for those who want it.
6. **On the water (or imagined field test)**
    - If you’ve fished it since: a few lines about how it behaved and how the fix felt in real use.
    - If not yet: “This reel’s next stop is the river. I’ll report back in a Reel Stories post once it’s had a proper day out.”
7. **Closing**
    - One closing line like: “Sometimes a sticky clicker is just old grease; sometimes it’s your chance to get to know a reel all over again.”

## Structure: First Service Guide

For the first **Service Guide** (for a specific model), you can use almost the same skeleton every time so readers know what to expect.

This ended up being different--the service guide posts are just brief notifications that a new guide stack has been published on the repo and on YouTube.

**Title example:**
“Service Guide: [Brand] [Model] – Basic Tear‑Down and Clean”

Sections:

1. **Overview**
    - What this reel is, roughly when it’s from, why someone might still want to fish or restore it.
    - Difficulty rating (easy, moderate, fussy) and approximate time.
2. **Symptoms \& common issues**
    - Short list of the problems this guide focuses on (e.g., rough retrieve, weak drag, sticky clicker).
    - Mention anything this guide does *not* cover (e.g., full repainting, parts fabrication).
3. **Tools \& supplies**
    - Simple list: screwdrivers, picks, brushes, solvent, grease/oil, optional tools.
    - One line about safety / not losing springs and tiny parts.
4. **Exploded‑view / reference**
    - Either a photo or a simple description of the layout before disassembly.
    - Encourage readers to take their own photos as they go.
5. **Step‑by‑step tear‑down**
    - Numbered steps, each with 1–3 sentences.[^6_6]
    - Note any “watch this part” moments (springs that like to launch, left‑hand threads, etc.).
6. **Cleaning \& inspection**
    - How you clean parts and what to look for (wear, cracks, corrosion).
    - When a part is “good enough” vs. should really be replaced.
7. **Re‑assembly \& lubrication**
    - Steps in reverse, with notes on where to grease/oil and where to stay dry.
    - Mention any torque/feel cues instead of precise specs (“just snug, not gorilla‑tight”).
8. **Function check**
    - A short checklist: smooth crank, clicker behavior, drag, free spool, etc.
    - What “normal” feels and sounds like for this model.
9. **Links \& notes**
    - Link to the corresponding GitHub entry if you have it, for people who want a printable or more technical view.
    - Any known variations of the model and how this guide applies (or doesn’t).

You can keep Bench Notes and Reel Stories free‑form, and let the Service Guides be where the structure lives.

## Fill‑in‑the‑blanks Service Guide template

Use this as a reusable pattern for each model. You can literally copy/paste and fill:

**Title:**
`Service Guide: [Brand] [Model] – [Short focus, e.g., Basic Tear‑Down and Clean / Fixing Rough Retrieve / Clicker and Drag Tune‑Up]`

***

**1. Overview**

- “[Brand] [Model] is a [size/type, e.g., small freshwater baitcaster / classic surf reel] from around [year/decade].”
- “You still see these on [where: eBay, flea markets, in tackle boxes that haven’t been opened since the 70s].”
- “This guide covers [scope: basic cleaning, lubrication, and minor adjustments] so you can [goal: fish it, sell it with a clear conscience, or just get it working again].”
- “Difficulty: [Easy / Moderate / Fussy]. Time: about [X] minutes if you’re taking your time.”

***

**2. Symptoms \& common issues**

- “This guide is aimed at reels that are showing:
    - [Symptom 1: e.g., rough or gritty retrieve]
    - [Symptom 2: weak drag / inconsistent drag]
    - [Symptom 3: sticky or hesitant clicker]”
- “It does *not* cover: [e.g., repainting, deep corrosion repair, replacing missing parts].”

***

**3. Tools \& supplies**

- “[Tool list: small flathead/Phillips screwdrivers, parts tray, pick or dental tool, small brush, cotton swabs, solvent (e.g., mineral spirits or your choice), reel grease, reel oil].”
- “Optional but nice to have: [e.g., magnifier, digital camera or phone for reference photos, torque screwdriver if you’re fancy].”
- “Work over a tray or light‑colored towel so tiny springs and screws don’t get far.”

***

**4. Reference view**

- “Before you undo a single screw, take a photo of the reel from each side, and then of the internals once you remove the side plate.”
- “[Here include 1–2 labeled photos or a quick diagram: e.g., ‘Main gear, pinion, clicker, spring.’]”

***

**5. Step‑by‑step tear‑down**

Use numbered steps; example structure:

1. “Remove the [handle/side plate] by [action, e.g., backing out the two screws on the right side]. Set the screws in order.”
2. “Lift off the [side plate] and note how the [clicker spring / pawl] sits. Take a photo here—it’s your reassembly cheat sheet.”
3. “Continue with [next part]. Watch for [gotcha: e.g., spring under tension that wants to launch].”

Keep going until the reel is in the state you need for cleaning.

***

**6. Cleaning \& inspection**

- “Clean metal parts in [solvent] using [brush/swab]. Avoid soaking [parts that shouldn’t be soaked, e.g., certain plastics, painted surfaces].”
- “As you go, look for:
    - [Wear pattern A: flat spots on teeth, grooves, cracks]
    - [Wear pattern B: bent or tired springs]
    - [Corrosion spots that may need more than a wipe].”
- “Replace anything that is [criteria for “too far gone”]. If you can’t, note it; the reel may still fish fine with some quirks.”

***

**7. Re‑assembly \& lubrication**

- “Reassembly is tear‑down in reverse, but with fresh grease and oil in the right spots.”
- “Grease the [e.g., main gear teeth and drag surfaces] lightly—enough to coat, not cake.”
- “Oil the [e.g., bearings, spool shaft, pivot points] with a drop, not a puddle.”
- “Avoid grease on [parts that should stay dry for proper function].”
- “Tighten screws to ‘snug plus a hair’—firm, but not enough to strip old metal.”

***

**8. Function check**

- “After reassembly, check:
    - Handle turns smoothly, no grinding.
    - Spool spins freely with [expected] resistance.
    - Clicker engages cleanly and consistently.
    - Drag tightens and loosens smoothly through its range.”
- “Compare to how it felt before. Note anything still odd, and whether it’s a ‘live with it’ quirk or something to revisit.”

***

**9. Links \& notes**

- “A more technical, version‑tracked set of notes for this reel lives here: [GitHub repo link + specific file].”
- “If you’ve done a Reel Stories field test with this model, link to it here.”
- “If you spot a variation of this reel (different side plate, different drag stack), this guide should still help with [which parts apply], but always compare your reel to the photos as you go.”

## Simple caption ideas for bench and reel photos

You can keep captions short and plain; they don’t need to be clever every time. Here are a few plug‑and‑play styles:

### Bench photo captions

- “The bench on a good day: one reel, one problem, and just enough room left to work.”
- “Where most Vintage Reel Service Guides begin: tools, towels, and a reel in pieces.”
- “Today’s patient on the bench, before the first screw comes out.”
- “The basic setup for photographing and documenting reels as I go.”


### Reel / close‑up captions

- “The [Brand Model] as it arrived: dusty, a little gummy, but worth saving.”
- “Clicker assembly before cleanup. Old grease and a tired spring are already hinting at trouble.”
- “Same parts after cleaning and fresh lubrication—the difference is mostly feel.”
- “Everything laid out in order before going back into the reel.”

You can mix “narrative” captions (“On the bench, just before I discovered…”) with “reference” captions (“Main gear, pinion, and clicker parts laid out left to right”) depending on whether the image is more story or more guide.

### Using Substack as a shell (now)

While you’re focused on the repo, standards docs, and YouTube:

- Do the **bare‑minimum setup** on Substack: name, URL, logo/header, short About/description, and default settings.
- Create **one or two empty draft posts** using the outlines we sketched (“At the Bench,” “A Reel with a Sticky Clicker”) so Future You opens Substack and instantly sees where to start.
- Optionally set up a simple **welcome page / About** that states what the publication will be about and that content is coming soon; people who stumble in early at least know they’re in the right place.

You don’t need to publish anything until your repo and workflow feel solid; Substack doesn’t penalize you for taking your time, and most setup guides emphasize clarity of positioning over speed.
